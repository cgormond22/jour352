The Rise of Cancel Culture: A Force for Accountability or a Threat to Free Speech?

What is Cancel Culture?

Cancel culture has become the internet’s policing system throughout the past few years and users have mixed feelings about it. According to the Pew Research Center, “The phrase ‘cancel culture’ is said to have originated from a relatively obscure slang term – ‘cancel,’ referring to breaking up with someone – used in a 1980s song.” Nowadays, “breaking up with” online influencers has become a widespread practice.
Data from the Pew Research Center
If you have witnessed your favorite influencer’s follower count quickly decline or viewed an “apology video,” aimed at addressing a controversy surrounding them, you’ve likely encountered cancel culture.

Urban Outfitters

The cancellation of the clothing store, Urban Outfitters, is an example of a wide-scale cancellation. In the wake of the death of George Floyd and the Black Lives Matter Movement, many companies were making statements of anti-racism and voicing their support for the black community. Urban Outfitters’s controversial past began to come to light, with reports of racism among staff, underrepresentation of minorities and a history of producing offensive items of clothing. In a public statement, the company apologized to any employees or customers who felt offended or uncomfortable in their stores.
Urban Outfitters display in one of their thousands of stores. Photo credit: Sherry Li, The Daily Northwestern

Shane Dawson

Shane Dawson was once one of the most popular YouTubers, famous for his conspiracy theory videos, and loved by his millions of followers. However, the majority of younger generations could recall the infamous cancellation of Dawson. His downfall began when he posted a video making inappropriate gestures at a poster of then-11-year-old Willow Smith. Many who felt uncomfortable with this video, began digging into his past and found a plethora of “cancelable” content including problematic and offensive jokes, and even Dawson wearing blackface. Although he posted an apology video, attempting to dig himself out of the hole he was in, many of his past followers were not convinced. According to the Independent, days after releasing the apology video on June 28, 2020, Dawson lost more than 700,000 subscribers. Just over one week later, his subscriber count dropped by more than one million from 23.1 million to 22.1 million.
Shane Dawson in his apolofy video, "Taking Accountability." Photo credit: Variety

Ellen DeGeneres

Ellen DeGeneres is another public figure who went through a major cancellation in recent years. After a tweet in 2020 generated thousands of responses with stories of toxic environments while working for DeGeneres, the situation began to be investigated. Variety later published a report that crew members on “The Ellen DeGeneres Show” were “distressed and outraged” over treatment from top producers during the COVID-19 pandemic. Many reported that DeGeneres was an extremely harsh boss and was “demeaning” towards her coworkers. Although she issued many apologies, her talk show lost a significant amount of viewers in the following weeks, with a 43% decline, according to The New York Times. She later announced the permanent end to the nineteen-year-long show.

Is it Helpful or Hurtful?

Cancel culture has remained a controversial topic, with some believing that it is necessary to hold people accountable over the internet, and some believing that it serves as a way to hinder free speech and may lead to the spread of hate. The Pew Research Center cited many anonymous sources’ opinions on both sides of the spectrum. Accoridng to Pew, "Overall, 58% of U.S. adults say in general, calling out others on social media is more likely to hold people accountable, while 38% say it is more likely to punish people who don’t deserve it."
How does it hold people accountable?
  • “Silence tacitly condones the offensive action and encourages more of it.” — Man, 60s, Moderate Democrat
  • “I feel that no one should be able to post ANYTHING that is racist or hurtful to anyone regardless of their race, religion, color, ethnicity."— Woman, 50s, Democrat, no ideological leaning
  • “Social media makes it simple to share thoughts and ideas that are untrue, misleading or not well thought out. Calling out people holds them accountable to disseminate information that is accurate.” — Woman, 40s, Moderate Democrat
  • “They need to justify what they posted, which makes them think before posting.” — Woman, 30s, Conservative Republican
How does it hinder free speech?
  • “We have free speech in this country. People who say offensive things can be blocked. Policing speech is more terrifying than offensive speech, which leads to natural consequences. Most people will not listen to or follow a bigot. That’s the consequence, not the speech police.” — Woman, 60s, Moderate Republican
  • “Others are entitled to their opinion, and too often it isn’t about having a discussion but berating others for not agreeing with our point of view.” — Woman, 20s, Conservative Republican
  • “Freedom of speech. The cure for speech you do not like is more speech, not less. I may hate and disagree with everything you say. But I will fight to my death for your right to say it.” — Woman, 60s, Conservative Republican
  • “It’s a pack mentality, taking a minor offense and exploiting it to look good to the pack.” — Man, 70s, Conservative Republican

Has the Public Adjusted to Cancel Culture?

Cancel Culture has taught many users of social media apps that a digital footprint can truly last a lifetime. In numerous instances, including some highlighted in this article, public figures have been "canceled" for actions or comments made years earlier. This emphasizes the importance of exercising caution with online posts at all times. Despite this, Data For Progress has found that cancel culture has not largely affected the way that users behave online.

Data from Data for Progress

Moving Forward

As cancel culture continues to evolve, these questions about accountability, forgiveness, and the permanence of a digital footprint become more prevalent in society. As we have seen many believe it serves as a way to hold public figures and organizations accountable for harmful behavior. In contrast, others express concern that it may limit free speech, encourage a mob mentality, or spread negativity. What remains evident is that cancel culture will not disappear at any time soon. We must ensure that online discourse fosters constructive dialogue and mutual understanding.
